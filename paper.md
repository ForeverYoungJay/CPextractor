                  Single-crystal calcium fluoride (CaF2) is a key optical material in lithography system for advanced semiconductor manufacturing, yet its ultra-precision machining remains challenging due to its soft-brittle nature and pronounced crystallographic anisotropy. This paper systematically elucidates nanoscale incipient plasticity, deformation mechanism and crack formation of CaF2 under indentation, with particular emphasis on anisotropic effects, through a combined approach of experimental measurements and crystal plasticity finite element simulations. Results show that compared with the (111) sample, the (001) sample exhibited a lower pop-in load (0.44 mN vs. 1.18 mN) and smaller pop-in size (20.7 nm vs. 50.33 nm). Both slip trace patterns and crack initiation sites demonstrate strong crystallographic anisotropy. Quantitative analysis of accumulated shear strain within the {100}<110> slip systems determine the dominant slip systems responsible for the plastic flow, accounting for the observed anisotropic slip trace patterns. Furthermore, cracks on the (001) and (111) samples preferentially propagate along their respective dominant cleavage planes, as directly evidenced by the distributions of maximum principal stress and cleavage stresses on {111} planes. This work advances the mechanistic understanding of anisotropic, multi-scale indentation behaviour in brittle materials and offers valuable guidance for achieving high-quality ultraprecision manufacturing of CaF2.

Single-crystal calcium fluoride (CaF2 ) is a key optical material in lithography system for advanced semiconductor manufacturing, yet its ultra-precision machining remains challenging due to its soft-brittle nature and pronounced crystallographic anisotropy. This paper systematically elucidates nanoscale incipient plasticity, deformation mechanism and crack formation of CaF2  under indentation, with particular emphasis on anisotropic effects, through a combined approach of experimental measurements and crystal plasticity finite element simulations. Results show that compared with the (111) sample, the (001) sample exhibited a lower pop-in load (0.44 mN vs. 1.18 mN) and smaller pop-in size (20.7 nm vs. 50.33 nm). Both slip trace patterns and crack initiation sites demonstrate strong crystallographic anisotropy. Quantitative analysis of accumulated shear strain within the {100}<110> slip systems determine the dominant slip systems responsible for the plastic flow, accounting for the observed anisotropic slip trace patterns. Furthermore, cracks on the (001) and (111) samples preferentially propagate along their respective dominant cleavage planes, as directly evidenced by the distributions of maximum principal stress and cleavage stresses on {111} planes. This work advances the mechanistic understanding of anisotropic, multi-scale indentation behaviour in brittle materials and offers valuable guidance for achieving high-quality ultraprecision manufacturing of CaF2 .   Keywords Single-crystal CaF2   Crystal plasticity  Slip trace  Pop-in events  Crack mechanism    1 Introduction Single-crystal calcium fluoride (CaF2 ) serves as a key material for advanced lithography systems due to its superior physical and chemical properties, including excellent transmittance across the ultraviolet to infrared spectral range, high thermal stability, and chemical inertness [1 ,2 ]. The ultraprecise machining of CaF2  crystals to achieve surfaces with extremely low roughness and subsurface damage is fundamental for the performance of lithography systems [3 ,4 ]. However, the inherent brittleness and limited plasticity of CaF2  make it highly susceptible to crack initiation during mechanical processing, significantly restricting its machinability and reliability. An ideal approach to fabricate high-quality lenses is the ductile mode cutting [5 ,6 ], which relies on the stable plastic deformation to mechanically remove materials while avoiding crack formation. Both deformation and fracture behaviours of CaF2  are highly anisotropic owing to its intrinsic crystal structure, characterized by a face-centred cubic (FCC) lattice with a fluorite structure [7 ,8 ]. Therefore, understanding its deformation and fracture mechanisms is essential for enabling high-efficiency and low-damage fabrication processes. Indentation techniques [9 ,10 ] offer a powerful approach to systematically investigate the mechanical response of crystal materials across scales, such as the onset of plasticity (pop-in events) [11 ,12 ], the development of slip traces and the initiation of cracks [13 ,14 ], and their crystalline orientation-dependence [15 ,16 ]. During mechanical processing of brittle crystalline materials, the limited capacity to dissipate strain energy through plastic flow often results in the initiation and propagation of cracks [17 ,18 ]. To suppress the fracture crack and improve the machinability, it is of great importance to understand the fundamental mechanisms governing crack formation and propagation. Providing real-time insights into the stress distribution and the microstructural evolution remains a significant experimental challenge. To this end, researchers have explored various numerical simulations [19 ,20 ] and theoretical models [21 ,22 ]. Since the cracking behaviour is primarily governed by the stress field beneath the indenter, stress field models [23 ,24 ] have been extensively utilized to predict crack initiation and propagation. Xiao et al. [25 ] incorporated the indenter geometry into a stress model to predict crack initiation and propagation in fused silica. Zheng et al. [26 ] improved the elastic stress field model and used it to predict the crack behaviours of single-crystal silicon. Jiang et al. [27 ] employed a combined stress model to predict the nucleation and initial propagation of radial cracks during scratching single-crystal gallium nitride. On the other hand, some studies calculated the cleavage factors of typical cleavage planes and deduced the activation degree of cleavage fracture. Mizumoto et al. [28 ] evaluated cleavage factors to identify the preferred cleavage planes in single-crystal CaF2  under scratching. Piao et al. [29 ] analysed the anisotropic cracking of single-crystal MgF2  by evaluating cleavage factors on its typical {110} planes. However, theoretical models and cleavage parameters usually rely on idealized or simplified loading conditions, and typically neglect critical microstructural features, such as grain boundaries and slip system interactions. Therefore, recently, numerical simulations, such as molecular dynamics (MD) and finite element modelling (FEM), have been increasingly employed to investigate the cracking behaviour of brittle crystalline materials. Yin et al. [30 ] using FEM investigated surface and subsurface fracture in polycrystalline YAG ceramics under quasi-static indentation. Zhao et al. [31 ] utilized MD simulations with the Vashishta potential to explore the grain boundary-mediated fracture of polycrystalline 3C–SiC. Gao et al. [32 ] studied the mechanisms for crack initiation and propagation in monocrystalline 4H–SiC under single-grit grinding through an ultra-large-scale MD model. However, the fracture mechanism of CaF2  has rarely been investigated with explicit consideration of the inherent microstructural evolution and the stress distribution under realistically complex loading conditions. Moreover, the present simulation models are constrained either by limited simulation time and length scales or by their inability to fully account for the anisotropic nature of crystalline materials. These limitations reduce the effectiveness of theoretical models and numerical simulations in capturing the full complexity of fracture mechanisms. Before cracks form and propagate, the material typically relies on plastic deformation, primarily through the activation of specific slip systems, to accommodate the applied stress [33 ,34 ]. Such plastic deformation plays a crucial role in dissipating strain energy, and enhancing it can effectively delay crack initiation [35 ,36 ]. Therefore, elucidating the plastic deformation mechanisms in CaF2  is crucial for understanding its pre-fracture response and providing guidance for mitigating crack initiation. The associated severe slip shear led to prominent slip traces observable on the free surface [37 ], which are also found in other brittle crystalline materials, such as MgF2  [38 ] and p-ZnSe [39 ]. Some studies have attempted to unravel the slip mechanism of single-crystal CaF2 . Guo et al. [40 ] analysed the geometry between the scratched surface and slip planes to elucidate slip trace formation in single-crystal CaF2 . Mizumoto et al. [28 ,41 ] quantitatively analysed the plastic deformation behaviour of single-crystal CaF2  during plunge-cut tests by computing deformation parameters weighted according to the Schmid factors of both primary and secondary slip systems. Utilizing these calculations, they predicted the activation tendencies of individual slip systems in CaF2  single-crystals with different orientations. Niki et al. [42 ] used a simplified geometrical model based on the intersection between the potential slip planes and the loaded crystal plane to explain the occurrence of slip steps on the indented CaF2  surface. Unravelling the deformation mechanism behind slip trace and their dependence on crystallographic anisotropy is crucial for a deep understanding of CaF2 's mechanical behaviour and for advancing strategies to enhance its ductile deformation. However, to date, interpretations of these slip traces have mainly relied on Schmid factors analysis or simplified geometric relationships between slip planes and loading directions. Such approaches tend to oversimplify the deformation process and neglect critical factors such as the non-uniform stress distribution, local lattice rotation, and hardening interactions among slip systems. Prior to the plastic shear slip, the material responds elastically during the initial stage of indentation [43 ]. When the local shear stress reaches a critical threshold, dislocation loops nucleate and propagate rapidly, giving rise to sudden displacement bursts in the load-displacement curves, commonly referred to as pop-in events [11 ,44 ]. The pop-in events, as the signature of the onset of plastic deformation, reflect the material's resistance to yielding under localized stress [45 ,46 ]. P. Sadrabadi et al. [47 ] employed nanoindentation tests and etch pit techniques to demonstrate that pop-in events in CaF2  nanoindentation directly correlate with dislocation nucleation and that the ensuing density gradients lead to the indentation size effect (ISE). More recently, increasing attention has been paid to the factors influencing the incipient plasticity of CaF2  under indentation. Lodes et al. [48 ] investigated the influence of the pre-existing dislocation microstructure on the pop-in behaviour of CaF2  and revealed that the presence of dislocations significantly lowers the pop-in load and alleviates the ISE. Chua et al. [49 ] demonstrated that elevated temperatures reduce the pop-in load of CaF2  by promoting dislocation nucleation. The effect of water on the indentation behaviour of CaF2  was studied by Zhan et al. [50 ], reporting that water reduces the pop-in load. In addition, Aruga et al. [51 ] reported that the mechanical properties of CaF2 , including hardness and Young's modulus, exhibit orientation-dependent behavior. However, despite the important role of orientation in governing pop-in behavior, its influence has received relatively little attention in previous studies. A comprehensive understanding of how different crystallographic orientations affect the occurrence and characteristics of the intermittent plasticity is therefore essential for accurately interpreting the mechanical response of CaF2 . In summary, during the indentation of brittle crystals [20 ,52 ], the material accommodates the progressively imposed deformation through a sequential response that begins with elastic response, followed by pop-in events that signal the onset of plasticity, and the activation of shear slip systems, ultimately leading to crack initiation, as illustrated in Fig. 1  . However, a systematic and cross-scale investigation of the indentation response of single-crystal CaF2 , particularly its dependence on crystallographic orientation, is still lacking. Specifically, the exact physical origins of fracture cracks and slip traces, representing the dominant microstructural evolution underlying the brittle and ductile responses of CaF2 , remain unclear. This is primarily due to the lack of comprehensive consideration of key intrinsic factors such as lattice rotation, intrinsic crystal structure, and microstructure interactions. In addition, the effect of crystal orientation on the nanoscale deformation events, i.e., pop-in and ISE, remains underexplored. To this end, this study aims to elucidate the deformation and fracture mechanisms of single-crystal CaF2  throughout the entire indentation process, with special emphasis on the effect of crystallographic orientation. Indentation experiments were conducted on representative crystal planes (111) and (001) of single-crystal CaF2 . Crystal plasticity finite element (CPFE) model, explicitly considering the specific slip and cleavage systems of CaF2 , was developed and validated against experimental measurements. By the combination of experiments and CPFE simulations, the underlying mechanisms of anisotropic plastic deformation and crack initiation were elucidated by systematically quantifying the orientation-dependent activation of inherent slip systems and the cleavage planes. In addition, the pop-in events, in terms of pop-in load and size, and ISE were captured and fitted through theoretical model, showing distinct anisotropic characteristics. This work will deepen the mechanistic understanding of anisotropic, scale-spanning indentation responses in brittle materials and facilitate the advancement of high-quality processing of anisotropic brittle crystals.  2 Methodology 2.1 Materials and experimental conditions To elucidate the anisotropy of the indentation response, indentation tests were performed on the (111) and (001) crystal planes, which represent two characteristic orientations of CaF2 . The (111) plane is recognized as the principal cleavage plane, while the (001) plane serves as the primary slip plane for CaF2  [53 ,54 ], thereby enabling a direct comparison of their distinct deformation and fracture behaviours. All the CaF2  single crystal specimens used in this study were obtained from the same growth batch to ensure consistency. The specimens with dimensions of 10 mm × 10 mm × 1 mm have been chemo-mechanically polished to achieve a surface roughness less than 15 Å. These crystals exhibit very high optical quality, with an internal transmittance exceeding 99.8 % at 193 nm, as reported by the material supplier, which indicates minimal defects and high crystallographic uniformity. The crystallographic directions of the specimens were determined by X-ray diffraction (XRD) to ensure an orientation accuracy of ±1°. The phase purity and lattice integrity of the specimens were further confirmed by Raman spectroscopy. As shown in Fig. 2  (a), specimens show the characteristic T2 g Raman mode of CaF2  at ≈321 cm−1 , and no secondary Raman bands were detected within the measured spectral range. Indentation tests on single-crystal CaF2  were performed using the indenter (Agilent Nano Indenter G200) equipped with a Berkovich diamond tip of tip radius ∼200 nm. Before the indentation tests, the projected area of the Berkovich indenter was calibrated by curve fitting the A-hc  data (i.e., the contact area versus contact depth) to the function, A=m 0  h c  2  +m 1  h c   + m 2  h c  1/2  +m 3  h c  1/4  +m 4  h c  1/8  . The calibration was carried out through a series of nanoindentation tests on a fused silica reference sample provided by the indenter manufacturer. Fig. 2 (b) presents the schematic of indentation tests. As the indenter geometry can affect the indentation response of crystalline materials [55 ], one edge of the Berkovich indenter was aligned parallel to the crystallographic directions [1 ‾  1 ‾     2] and [100] for the (111)- and (001)-oriented surfaces, respectively. Maximum indentation depths are set to 2000 nm, 3000 nm, and 4000 nm, and for each depth, three indents were performed to ensure experimental repeatability. Continuous stiffness measurement (CSM) mode was employed to obtain the load-displacement response. By superimposing a small high-frequency oscillation onto the quasi-static loading signal, this technique enables a continuous evaluation of hardness throughout the indentation process. Besides, as it involves a single continuous loading process, CSM mode effectively minimizes cyclic fatigue and associated damage, thereby ensuring more reliable measurement of plastic deformation and initiation of cracks compared to cyclic indentation. The loading rate was set to 0.05 s−1  followed by a 5 s dwell period at peak load to minimize the influence of thermal drift. All the indentation tests were performed under stable laboratory conditions with relative humidity maintained at approximately 40 % and temperature at about 293K. After indentation tests, the surface morphologies of the indent imprints were examined using SEM (Sigma 500, ZEISS). Prior to SEM observation, the indented CaF2  specimens were sputter-coated with a thin Pt layer (∼5 nm) to mitigate charging effects during imaging.  2.2 CPFE simulation and model verification The crystal plasticity kinematic framework adopted in this study was originally introduced by Asaro [56 ] and Rice [57 ]. A detailed description of the crystal plasticity constitutive relations is provided in supplementary materials. A three-dimensional crystal plasticity finite element (CPFE) model was developed to simulate the anisotropic deformation and crack behaviour of single-crystal CaF2  under indentation. The model (Fig. 3  (a)) was with dimensions of 64 μm × 64 μm × 32 μm with C3D8R type of elements. To balance the simulation efficiency and accuracy, as shown in Fig. 3 (c), refined meshes were seeded in the vicinity of the contact region, while a gradually coarser mesh was applied towards the far field. The indenter was modelled as a rigid body with a blunt tip radius of approximately 200 nm (Fig. 3 (b)), consistent with the experimental setup. The contact between indenter and specimen was assumed frictionless, as the friction coefficient was found to have a negligible effect on the shear strain and stress distributions, as demonstrated in Figs. S1 and S2  of the Supplementary materials. The specimen bottom was fixed to avoid rigid motion, while the remaining surfaces were treated as free surfaces. Fig. 3 (d) depicts the crystallographic orientation of two indented planes. The constitutive behaviour of CaF2  was described by a rate-dependent crystal plasticity formulation, considering primary slip systems {100}⟨110⟩ and the potential cleavage planes {111} [58 ,59 ], as illustrated in Fig. 4  . Table 1   lists the six slip systems in single-crystal CaF2 . The related material parameters were initially adopted from previous studies [60 ,61 ], and further calibrated using a semi-empirical fitting approach. During calibration, τ 0   and n , identified as the two most influential parameters in the CPFE framework, were iteratively tuned until the simulated load-displacement curves closely matched the experimental results. Subsequent sensitivity analysis further demonstrated that the optimal parameters were well constrained within narrow bounds ([80, 90] MPa for τ 0   and [13 ,17 ] for n ), confirming both the identifiability and reliability of the calibration. The employed parameters are listed in Table 2  . Fig. 5   presents a comparison between the simulated and experimental load–displacement curves for indentations on the (111) and (001) planes, where the close agreement demonstrates the accuracy of the CPFE model. Furthermore, as detailed in Section 3.2 and 3.3 , the spatial distributions of accumulated shear strain, maximum principal stress, and cleavage stress from the simulations agree well with experimental observations of slip trace locations and crack initiation sites, confirming the model's capability to capture the anisotropic deformation and fracture behaviour of single-crystal CaF2  under indentation.   3 Result and discussion 3.1 Anisotropic pop-in and ISE Fig. 5  presents the load-depth responses of (111)- and (001)-oriented single-crystal CaF2  during indentation at various maximum indentation depths, conducted in constant stiffness mode to ensure stable contact compliance during loading. At the depth of 2000 nm, 3000 nm and 4000 nm, the (111)-oriented sample exhibits loads of 173 mN, 350 mN, and 558 mN, while the (001)-oriented sample shows lower values of 131 mN, 240 mN, and 345 mN, respectively. The lower forces required to achieve the same indentation depths for the (001) sample highlight the crystallographic dependence of mechanical response. This behaviour suggests that the (001) orientation exhibits a reduced resistance to deformation (i.e., lower hardness) and a greater tendency for slip system activation, which may also lead to earlier onset of pop-in events during nanoindentation. In order to elucidate the effects of crystallographic anisotropy on the nanoindentation response of CaF2 , detailed inspection of the load-displacement curves at nanoscale was performed. Fig. 6   shows the representative zoomed-in P-h  curves for the (111)- and (001)-oriented samples, respectively. Distinct pop-in events, which are typically characterized by sudden, discontinuous increases in penetration depth occurring at nearly constant or slightly increasing applied load, are observed on both crystallographic orientations. The elastic contact behaviour, prior to pop-in, was often analysed by isotropic Hertzian fitting [62 ]. According to the Hertzian contact theory, the relationship between the applied load P  and the indentation depth h  in the purely elastic regime is described by:(1) F = 4 3  E r  R  h 1.5      where E r   and R  are the reduced elastic modulus and the radius of the spherical indenter, respectively. Notably, the Berkovich indenter deviates from the assumptions of Hertzian contact due to its sharp, pyramidal geometry, especially at indentation depths beyond ∼0.1 R . In such cases, the load-depth response is better described by Kick's law [63 ,64 ]:(2) F = C h 2      where C  is a fitting constant. As shown in Fig. 6 (a and b), the initial loading segments exhibit good agreement with Kick's law, reflecting purely elastic behaviour. However, after the onset of pop-in events, significant deviations from this quadratic relation are observed, indicating the initiation of plastic deformation. To quantitatively analyse the pop-in phenomena, two key parameters are extracted: the pop-in load, defined as the load at which the displacement jump initiates, and the pop-in size, referring to the magnitude of the sudden increase in penetration depth. Fig. 6 (c) presents the pop-in features in both samples, indicating that the earlier pop-in occurs, the smaller its size. Besides, it is worth noting that the loads at which the first pop-in event occurred varied slightly among repeated indentations, despite nominally identical test conditions [45 ], due to the stochastic nature of dislocation nucleation. As such, statistical analysis of the first pop-in events was performed based on repeated nanoindentation tests. The corresponding values are listed in Table 3  . For the (001)-oriented sample, the mean pop-in load was measured to be approximately 0.44 mN, with a standard deviation of 0.02 mN. In contrast, the (111) surface exhibited a higher mean pop-in load of approximately 1.18 mN, accompanied by a slightly wider distribution (standard deviation: 0.22 mN). The pop-in size was also found to be orientation-dependent. The (111)-oriented sample showed a larger average pop-in size of 50.33 nm (standard deviation: 8.31 nm), whereas the (001)-oriented sample displayed a smaller average pop-in size of 20.7 nm with reduced variability. These differences indicate that the (001) sample is more prone to earlier plastic events, consistent with its lower indentation resistance. As further illustrated in Fig. 6 (d and e), the box plots of the pop-in size and load corroborate the pronounced orientation sensitivity. Moreover, the 95 % confidence intervals (CIs) and effect sizes were evaluated to quantify the orientation dependence of the pop-in behaviour. The 95 % CIs of the pop-in size are [20.494, 22.907] nm for the (001) sample and [43.561, 57.107] nm for the (111) sample, and the corresponding intervals of the pop-in load are [0.417, 0.455] mN and [0.998, 1.357] mN, respectively. The large effect sizes (4.524 for pop-in size and 4.471 for pop-in load) quantitatively demonstrate an extremely strong orientation dependence in the incipient plasticity. In CSM loading mode, the material hardness was continuously determined by dividing the instantaneous load by the corresponding contact area, with the contact stiffness measured via a superimposed oscillatory load. With further indenter penetration, severe slip activity is activated to accommodate the increasing strain, resulting in discernible slip traces on the indentation surface. This section focuses on analysing the effect of crystal orientation on the slip trace patterns in CaF2  and elucidating the underlying deformation mechanisms. Figs. 8 and 9    display SEM images of the indentation impression on both (111) and (001) samples at a maximum indentation depth of approximately 4500 nm, where distinct slip traces are evident. For clarity, the three indentation facets are labelled as Facet A, B and C. For the (111) sample, three distinct types of slip traces are identified, as shown in Fig. 8 (b–d). Type I slip traces are observed on Facet A and align parallel to crystallographic direction [1 1 ‾     0]. On Facet B, the primary slip traces (Type II) are inclined at approximately 45° to the [1 1 ‾     0] direction, while slip traces on Facet C (type III) exhibit a symmetrical pattern to Type II with respect to the [1 ‾  1 ‾     2] crystal direction. Compared to (111) sample, slip traces on the (001) sample (Fig. 9 ) can be categorized into two main types based on their general orientations. One type, aligned parallel to the [010] crystallographic direction is observed on Facet A in Fig. 9 (c). The other type, localized on Facets B and C (Fig. 9 (d and e)), is oriented roughly perpendicular to the [010] direction, with a slight angular deviation on each facet. The distinct slip trace patterns of the (001) and (111) samples underscore the strong orientation dependence of deformation in CaF2 , indicating different underlying slip activation mechanisms. To elucidate the underlying deformation mechanism, the accumulative shear strain on typical slip planes of CaF2 , i.e., {100} slip planes, were derived based on CPFE simulations. As shown in Fig. 10  , the equivalent plastic strain is primarily confined to the residual impression, indicating that plastic deformation is concentrated beneath and around the indenter. The accumulative shear strain exhibits pronounced localization and crystallographic anisotropy. For (111)-oriented indentation (Fig. 10 (a2-a4)), accumulative shear strain of the (001) slip plane predominantly distributes along Facet A of the indentation imprint. In contrast, the (100) and (010) planes exhibit dominant shear activities along Facet B and Facet C, respectively. For (001)-oriented sample (Fig. 10 (b2-b4)), shear strain on the (100) slip plane is primarily concentrated along Facet A, whereas the (010) slip plane exhibits significant shear activity along both Facets B and C. Shear strain associated with the (001) slip plane remains small across all facets, implying a relatively low level of activation under this loading orientation. This spatial partitioning of shear localization is closely associated with the orientation of the active slip systems with respect to the local stress field imposed by the indenter geometry. Lattice rotation affects the relative orientation between slip systems and the applied stress. As shown in Fig. 11  , the surface lattice rotations are more pronounced in both samples, facilitating the accommodation of the indenter. In addition, the accumulative shear strain on the (001), (010) and (100) slip planes represents the intensity of slip activities on each slip planes. As such, by projecting the intersection lines between the activated slip planes and the sample surface, the predicted potential slip traces (marked as red lines in Fig. 10 (c and d)) on both indented crystal planes are obtained. These predicted slip traces are consistent with the experimentally observed surface slip features, thereby confirming the capability of the CPFE model to accurately reproduce the crystallographic slip behaviours and surface slip trace patterns during indentation. To gain deeper insight into the deformation mechanisms, it is essential to further evaluate the activation of individual slip systems. As the accumulated shear strain serves as a quantitative indicator of slip activity [67 ,68 ], the strain distributions of six primary slip systems for both indentation orientations, derived from simulations, are shown in Fig. 12  . For the (111)-oriented sample, slip systems 1# , 3# , and 5#  are intensively activated, with intensive shear strain localized along Facets A, B, and C of the indentation imprint, respectively. In contrast, slip systems 2# , 4# , and 6#  exhibit negligible activation. It indicates that slip systems 1# , 3# , and 5#  are the dominant slip systems under this crystallographic orientation. In contrast to the (111)-oriented case, where deformation is governed by a few dominant slip systems, the (001)-oriented sample exhibits activation across all slip systems, indicating that plastic deformation proceeds through a multi-slip system coordination. The most pronounced slip shear occurs on slip systems 1# , 2# , 3#  and 4# , whereas relatively weak slip occurs on slip systems 5#  and 6# .  3.3 Effect of crystal orientation on crack formation For brittle materials, once the local stress surpasses the fracture toughness, cracks are inevitably initiated to release the excessive strain energy, complementing the plastic deformation. In this section, the anisotropic crack patterns observed in indentation experiments are analysed and further interpreted through CPFE simulations to uncover the underlying fracture mechanisms. Indentation-induced cracks were observed on both (111)- and (001)-oriented surfaces when the indentation depth reached 4000 nm. Fig. 8(a) and 9(a-b)  present indentation-induced crack morphologies on the (111)- and (001)-oriented surfaces. For the (111) sample, crack initiation occurs adjacent to facets B and C of the indentation imprint, while for the (001) sample, cracks tend to initiate near facet A. It suggests a strong dependence of crack initiation sites on crystallographic orientation. Crack initiation is closely governed by the local stress state, particularly the maximum principal stress σ1 [23 ,69 ]. To reveal the mechanistic origins of the orientation-dependent surface fracture, the fracture parameter <σ1> during the indentation process were calculated from the CPFE simulations. According to the maximum principal stress theory, cracks in brittle materials are initiated once the <σ1> exceed the critical threshold. Here, the symbol <•> denote the Macaulay bracket, i.e., <σ1> = 0, if σ1<0 and <σ1> = σ1 if σ1>0. This operation ensures that only tensile stresses are considered in fracture analysis, as compressive stresses do not contribute to crack initiation in brittle materials. To obtain σ1 at each material point, it is necessary to analyse the full local stress state represented by the Cauchy stress tensor σij . By diagonalizing this tensor, i.e., transforming it into the principal stress frame where all shear components vanish, the three principal stresses σ1, σ2 and σ3 can be obtained, with σ1 being the maximum. These principal values are derived by solving the characteristic equation of the stress tensor,(4) σ 3  − ( σ 11  + σ 22  + σ 33   )  σ 2  + ( σ 11  σ 22  + σ 22  σ 33  + σ 33  σ 11  − σ 12 2  − σ 23 2  − σ 31 2   )  σ − ( σ 11  σ 22  σ 33  + 2 σ 12  σ 23  σ 31  − σ 11  σ 23 2  − σ 22  σ 31 2  − σ 33  σ 12 2   )  = 0      Based on the above equations, the fracture parameter < σ1> was extracted from the simulation results to evaluate the possibility of crack initiation. Fig. 13(a) and 14(a)    show the distributions of < σ1> on the (111)- and (001)-oriented surfaces. The distribution of the normal stresses along the x, y, and z directions (i.e., σ11 , σ22  and σ33 ) are also presented to illustrate their contribution to the principal tensile stress. It is evident that the distribution of fracture parameter <σ1> exhibits strong crystallographic orientation dependence. Notably, the predicted fracture parameter distribution from the simulations closely matches the crack locations observed experimentally. Specifically, the (111) sample exhibits the elevated fracture possibility near Facets B and C, while on the (001) sample, high values of fracture parameter concentrate on the vicinity of Facet A. To identify the predominant cleavage planes for each orientation, the resolved normal stresses perpendicular to each potential cleavage plane are calculated within the CPFE model by the following equation,(5) σ n  = σ 11  n 1 2  + σ 22  n 2 2  + σ 33  n 3 2  + 2 ( σ 12  n 1  n 2  + σ 13  n 1  n 3  + σ 23  n 2  n 3   )      where n i   represents the component of cleavage plane normal. The inserts of Figs. 15 and 16    present the distributions of resolved normal stresses on four cleavage planes. To enable a more direct and quantitative comparison, the resolved stress values were also extracted along specific paths around three facets, and the paths are marked as dark lines in inserts of Fig. 15 and 16 . On the (111)-oriented surface (Fig. 15 ), cleavage plane (111) is barely activated. Cleavage plane (1 ‾  1 ‾     1) shows pronounced stress concentrations near Facets B and C. Cleavage planes (1 ‾  1 1 ‾    ) and (1 1 ‾  1 ‾    ) are predominantly activated near Facets B and C, respectively, with both also exhibiting weaker activation near Facet A. In contrast, on the (001) surface (Fig. 16 ), all four cleavage planes are activated mainly near Facet A, with cleavage planes (111) and (1 ‾  1 1 ‾    ) showing the highest stress levels. The resolved stress distributions align well with both the fracture parameter fields and the experimentally observed crack locations, supporting that the cleavage activation governs the anisotropic crack morphologies. Also, it can be inferred that (1 ‾  1 ‾     1), (1 ‾  1 1 ‾    ) and (1 1 ‾  1 ‾    ) are the dominant cleavage planes responsible for crack initiation on the (111)-oriented surface, while (111) and (1 ‾  1 1 ‾    ) are predominant on the (001)-oriented surface.   4 Conclusions This work presents a comprehensive investigation into the anisotropic characteristics of pop-in events, plastic deformation, and fracture behaviour of CaF2  by conducting indentation tests on (001)- and (111)- oriented single-crystal CaF2 . To reveal the intrinsic deformation and fracture mechanisms, the CPFE model was developed by incorporating both principal slip systems {100} <110> and cleavage planes {111}, and the activation of slip shear and cleavage planes were explicitly captured. It should be noted that, since the current CPFE framework does not incorporate an explicit dislocation nucleation mechanism, it is intended to provide mechanistic insights into the post-pop-in plastic deformation and crack initiation, rather than the incipient plasticity. The cross-scale indentation responses of CaF2 , including pop-in events, ISE, slip activity and crack initiation, exhibit a strong crystallographic orientation dependence. The primary outcomes are summarized as follows:• The (111) sample exhibits higher mean pop-in loads and larger pop-in sizes compared to the (001) sample. Besides, both samples show pronounced ISE, especially when indentation depth is less than 400 nm.  • The plastic deformation flow in (111)-oriented sample is primarily governed by slip systems (100)[011], (010)[101] and (001)[110], while (001) orientation activates all slip systems, promoting coordinated multi-slip deformation. The orientation-dependence of slip activation leads to the anisotropic pattern of slip traces.  • Crack initiation sites can be identified from the fields of maximum principal stress and cleavage stress. For (111) sample, cracks preferentially initiate near Facets B and C, governed by the activation of cleavage planes (1 ‾  1 ‾     1), (1 ‾  1 1 ‾    ) and (1 1 ‾  1 ‾    ). In contrast, for (001) sample, cracks tend to initiate near Facet A, primarily controlled by cleavage planes (111) and (1 ‾  1 1 ‾    ).
